What does it mean?

Tom and Sara use relative clauses throughout the conversation. Look at the highlighted parts.
Tom says "the film that won the festival prize." Which film does he mean? Is it just an ordinary film?
No. It is the film that won the prize.
Sara says "the director, who studied in New York." Did we learn extra information about the director?
Yes. We learned that the director studied in New York.
Tom says "the café where they first met." Can you remove those words and still understand which café?
No. Without it, we cannot know which café he means.

Two types

Defining

  • No commas
  • who / that / which / whose / where
  • Essential — meaning changes if removed
  • The film that won the prize was amazing.

Non-defining

  • Commas required
  • who / which / whose (never "that")
  • Extra info — sentence works without it
  • The director, who studied in New York, was great.
🧪 Quick test: Can you remove the clause and still know which person or thing?  Yes → use commas.  No → no commas.

Why do we use it?

"The soundtrack, which was composed in just two weeks, was beautiful."
Why and where do we use relative clauses?
To add information about a noun — right after it, in the same sentence.
